# Data provenance

This document inventories every data asset that ships in the
`habesha-names` wheel, where it came from, and who verified it. It is
written for compliance review: if you are evaluating this library for
KYC/AML, remittance, or HR use, this is the chain of custody for the
linguistic data your pipeline would depend on.

**No scraped personal data.** The lexicon entries are common-name
*reference* data — the kind of information found in any discussion of
Ethiopian and Eritrean naming — not records of individuals. The repository
contains no scraping or data-collection code, and the library makes no
network calls at runtime or at build time (data generation uses only the
Python standard library's bundled Unicode database).

## The `verified` workflow

Every linguistic data entry carries a `"verified"` boolean (contract:
[`src/habesha_names/data/schema.md`](src/habesha_names/data/schema.md)).
Entries were seeded during development by the coding agent with
`"verified": false`; only Robel Shemeles (native speaker) flips a flag to
`true`, after reviewing the entry. All entries currently shipped in the
three lexicon files were reviewed and flipped to `"verified": true` in
task-3b (2026-07-14, review recorded in `review_report.txt` and the
project's internal decisions log). Newly added entries start `false`
again until reviewed.

recorded in the project's internal decisions log. Changing a table cell is a
linguistic decision, not a refactor.

## Not in the wheel

The golden test corpus (`tests/golden/pairs.json`) ships only in the sdist,
for running the test suite. It is mechanically generated by
`scripts/gen_golden_pairs.py` from the lexicon plus rule-based respellings;
agent-seeded pairs carry `"needs_human": true` markers and the corpus is
pending human curation. It is not used at runtime.

## External corpora (not in the repo, not in the wheel)

Since 2026-07-17 the project uses third-party name datasets as **local
evaluation and review-prioritization corpora**. The boundary is strict, and
it is the reason the "no scraped personal data" statement above stays true:

- The corpora live only in the maintainer's gitignored `data-lab/`
  directory. They are never committed, never in the sdist or wheel, and
  never redistributed. `data-lab/README.md` (local-only) records each
  corpus's URL, retrieval date, sha256, and license status.
- The first such corpus (`lehagere/ethiopian-names` on GitHub) has **no
  license** and derives from Ethiopia's published 40/60 housing-lottery
  winner lists — aggregated to given-name → occurrence-count before it
  reaches us, so it holds no full-name records, but its origin and OCR
  noise make it unshippable on both provenance and quality grounds. It is
  treated accordingly: as a prioritization signal, not as data.
- Two committed scripts consume it, offline only (no network calls):
  [`scripts/eval_corpus.py`](scripts/eval_corpus.py) measures how much
  real-world spelling variation the lexicon + variant engine already cover,
  and [`scripts/mine_candidates.py`](scripts/mine_candidates.py) emits a
  frequency-ranked *review queue* (`data-lab/reports/candidates.json`,
  local-only) of names and spellings worth reviewing next.
- Nothing moves from a corpus into `src/habesha_names/data/` mechanically.
  A mined candidate becomes a lexicon entry only when Robel authors it —
  fidel spelling, canonical Latin, gender, origin — through the normal
  `verified` workflow above. Corpus counts and `_review` metadata never
  enter the shipped files; the loader's exact-key validation would reject
  them by construction.
- Websites with meanings/etymologies (e.g. Madote, Behind the Name) are
  consulted-reference material for the human reviewer, like a dictionary;
  their tables are not extracted, and their content is not shipped.

In short: external corpora tell us *what to review*; they are never the
source of what we ship.
